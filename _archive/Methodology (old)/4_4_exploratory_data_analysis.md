# 4.4 Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)

This section documents the empirical characteristics of the assembled dataset before formal econometric analysis. The aim is to describe the structure, distributions, and cross-market patterns of the key variables—prices, volumes, funding rates, and prediction market probabilities—without drawing economic conclusions or reporting statistical test results. The discussion is organized into three subsections: price dynamics across markets, liquidity and trading activity, and prediction market probability behaviour. All descriptions refer to the finalized, aligned dataset and to the diagnostics produced as part of the pipeline. No regression output or formal hypothesis tests are included; the section is purely descriptive and preparatory.

---

## 4.4.1 Price Dynamics Across Markets

The relationship between **spot price, futures price, and index price** is examined at 5-minute frequency. Visual inspection of time series plots shows that the three series move closely together over the sample window, with the perpetual futures price and the index price tracking the spot price with only minor deviations. The **basis** (the spread between the perpetual futures price and the spot or index price) is computed and its behaviour over time is documented. The basis fluctuates around zero or a small positive or negative level, with episodes of wider spread that may coincide with funding dates or periods of elevated volatility. **Mark price versus index price** are compared to assess consistency; in normal conditions the mark price remains close to the index, with larger gaps appearing only in short windows around funding or sharp moves. These patterns are documented via time series plots and summary statistics; no causal or structural interpretation is offered.

**Cross-correlation patterns** are examined at a high level. Correlation heatmaps are produced for spot returns, futures returns, index returns, and (where applicable) funding rate and basis measures over the 5-minute grid. The heatmaps reveal the degree of linear association between series; typically, spot and futures returns are highly correlated, and the index aligns with both. **Stability of 5-minute returns** is assessed descriptively: the distribution of 5-minute log returns for spot and futures is inspected via histograms and basic moments. The bulk of mass is concentrated near zero, with tails that extend to larger positive and negative values; the shape is consistent with fat-tailed return distributions commonly reported for crypto assets. **Cross-correlation diagnostics** (e.g., correlograms or lead-lag correlation at a small number of lags) are produced for descriptive purposes only—to document how quickly one series co-moves with another at the chosen frequency. No formal tests of lead-lag or causality are performed in this section; the goal is only to characterise the data.

---

## 4.4.2 Liquidity and Trading Activity

**Volume distributions** in spot and futures are summarised. Histograms of 5-minute volume (in base or quote currency) show that most intervals have positive volume, with a right-skewed distribution: many intervals have moderate volume and a smaller number have very high volume. The share of intervals with zero or near-zero volume is reported; for the main spot and perpetual futures series used in the panel, this share is low enough to support continuous price and return series. **Funding rate variability** is described: the distribution of funding rate realisations over the sample is summarised (e.g., histogram or density), showing the range and typical magnitude of funding payments. Periods of sustained positive or negative funding are visible in time series plots and are noted as part of the descriptive record.

**Option trading activity and strike coverage** are documented. For the selected option expiries and near-ATM strike range, the number of contracts with non-zero volume over the sample and the distribution of volume across strikes are summarised. **Share of nonzero trading intervals** at 5-minute resolution is reported for options, indicating how often each contract or strike is updated. **Sparse trading behaviour in prediction markets** is described in detail: at many 5-minute grid points, no trade occurs for a given contract, so the raw probability series contains many missing values. Histogram diagnostics show the distribution of the number of observed probability updates per contract over the sample; some contracts update frequently while others update only occasionally. **Contract-level liquidity filtering results** are summarised: the number of contracts retained after applying minimum volume or activity thresholds, and the resulting **contract completeness statistics** (e.g., the proportion of grid points with at least one observation per contract, or the distribution of observation counts per contract). These statistics justify the decision to apply **forward-filling** only within contract: because updates are sparse and asynchronous, filling across contracts would be inappropriate, whereas within-contract forward-fill preserves the last known probability until the next trade. **Trade-bar share metrics** (e.g., the fraction of 5-minute bars with at least one trade) and **distributional properties** of volume and activity are reported where relevant. The discussion clarifies that liquidity conditions in prediction markets are such that a continuous probability series for econometric use requires a transparent, contract-specific fill rule, as implemented in the pipeline.

## 4.4.3 Prediction Market Probability Behaviour

The **structure of daily strike ladders** is described: for each expiry date in the sample, the set of strikes available and the number of contracts per expiry are reported. The ladders are balanced in the sense that a consistent strike range (e.g., around the prevailing price) is targeted across expiries, yielding a panel of contracts that are comparable across days. **Probability monotonicity across strikes** is examined: for a given expiry, the probability that the price is above strike X should weakly decrease as X increases. Diagnostics are produced to check whether **higher strikes consistently have lower probabilities**; any **monotonicity violations** (e.g., a higher strike with a higher implied probability than a lower strike at the same timestamp) are documented. No economic interpretation of such violations is offered; they are recorded as data characteristics. The **coverage percentages per contract**—the share of grid points in the contract’s lifetime for which a probability is observed (before any fill)—are summarised. **Probability bounds checks** are performed: all realised probability values are verified to lie in the unit interval [0, 1]. The **frequency of near-constant contracts**—contracts whose probability changes very little over the sample—is reported (e.g., via the distribution of within-contract standard deviation of probability). The **evolution of probability curves over time** is described: at selected timestamps, the cross-section of probabilities across strikes for a given expiry can be plotted, showing how the implied “curve” shifts as the spot price and time to expiry change. The discussion notes that these curves **resemble risk-neutral or belief distributions** over the strike dimension, with higher strikes typically associated with lower probabilities, consistent with the event “price above strike” being less likely for higher strikes. All of this is descriptive; no claim is made about market efficiency or rationality.

---

The exploratory data analysis confirms that the dataset exhibits **data integrity**: timestamps are aligned, duplicates are absent, and probability and price variables satisfy basic validity checks. **Liquidity** is sufficient for the chosen series—spot, futures, and the filtered option and prediction market contracts—to support meaningful time-series and panel analysis. The **panel structure** (long and wide formats, contract identifiers, and consistent 5-minute grid) is suitable for the correlation, Granger causality, arbitrage, and event analyses that follow. The next section proceeds to **probability construction and formal modeling**, building on this characterised dataset.
